<p><em>Fado</em> literally means &ldquo;destiny&rdquo; or &ldquo;fate&rdquo; and it is Portugal&rsquo;s national song.</p>
<p>Traditional Fado is sung to two classical and one Portuguese guitar. Guitar players are essential in this music genre. Carlos Paredes,&nbsp; (1925 &ndash;2004) born in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coimbra">Coimbra</a> and son of the equally famous&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artur_Paredes">Artur Paredes</a> is Portugal&rsquo;s most internationally renowned virtuoso guitar player. His talent continues alive and listening to his <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W2X_7Gi5krs">recordings</a> is a mesmerizing experience! </p>
<p>There are two different varieties: <a href="https://youtu.be/Vy4s8ZwQflk">Lisbon&rsquo;s Fado</a> has a more popular, down to earth style, often cheeky and convivial</p>
<p>in its Lisbon version, both male and female singers perform Fado; <a href="https://youtu.be/6KJTKnMJfZ8">Coimbra&rsquo;s Fado</a>, linked to the University, is usually sung my male students in their academic outfits. Although the most popular genre within Coimbra&rsquo;s Fado is the serenade, &lsquo;<em>a can&ccedil;&atilde;o de Coimbra&rsquo; </em>keeps reinventing itself by including new <a href="https://youtu.be/TNyjEQHXXj4">instruments</a> and <a href="https://youtu.be/omnW-DWz5IU">influences.</a> Don&rsquo;t miss the chance to experience this unique song while in Coimbra!</p>
